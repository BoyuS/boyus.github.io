title: How does the Fed influence federal funds rates?
---

<p align="justify">
* The (effective) federal funds rate, or the overnight rate, is the volume-weighted-average rate
at which depository institutions (usually commercial banks) borrow and lend their excess
reserves to each other overnight.
</p>


<p align="justify">
* The Fed requires banks to hold a certain percentage of their deposits in reserves at one of
the 12 regional federal reserve banks each night (the reserve requirement has been zero since
March 26, 2020). If they don’t have enough reserves, they could borrow what they need from
either other banks that hold excess reserves or the Federal Reserve discount window.
</p>

<p align="justify">
* The Fed started to pay interest on those reserves on Oct 1, 2008. I would focus on the Fed tools
after that (IORB and ON RRP framework). In short, the key rationale behind introducing
such a new framework is that the Fed has done too much quantitative easing (QE). The money
(reserve) supply curve has shifted too outward, making open market operations (buy/sell
treasuries) no longer a reliable tool to adjust federal funds rates. The open market operation
has turned into a long-term tool to maintain an ample supply of reserves.
</p>

<p align="justify">
So, back to the original question, how is the Fed able to ensure the federal funds rate is within
its target rate range? To understand this question, it’s critical to understand three concepts, IORB rate, ON RRP rate, and bank rate (discount rate). All three rates are quoted in the overnight
borrowing/lending markets.
</p>

<p align="justify">
* IORB rate (Interest on reserve balances): Interest paid on reserves.
</p>

<p align="justify">
* ON RRP rate (Overnight reverse repurchase agreement): ON RRP is an overnight transaction
in which the Fed sells a security to an eligible counterparty and simultaneously agrees to
buy the security back the next day, i.e. the Fed sells a repo. The ON RRP rate is the interest
rate offered by the Fed through such transaction (The actual rate is determined through an
auction. However since the Fed sets a very high capacity, the effective rate is close to the
offering rate). Different from the other two, the ON RRP opportunities are offered to a much
broader range of financial institutions, not just banks.
</p>

<p align="justify">
* Bank rate (or, discount rate): Bank rate is the rate of interest that the Fed charges on its loans through the Federal Reserve Discount Window. Different from the other two, this is the rate that the Fed offers for borrowing from them, not lending to (or depositing with) them.
</p>

<p align="justify">
How could these rates help control the federal funds rate?
</p>

<p align="justify">
* The Fed would set the bank rate around the upper bound of its target range. Therefore,
the federal funds rate would fall below the upper bound – if it breaches the upper bound,
everyone would directly borrow from the Fed, which reduces the borrowing demand and
thus the federal funds rate.
</p>

<p align="justify">
* ON RRP rate is set around the lower bound of its target range. Therefore, the federal funds
rate would stay above the lower bound. ON RRP serves as the minimum benefit a certain
institution could get from overnight. If an institution chooses to borrow money out instead
of staying with ON RRP, it would definitely require a higher rate.
</p>

<p align="justify">
* IORB rate is the main tool of the Fed. IORB serves as a guidance for the federal funds rate.
The thing is, banks could arbitrage the difference between IORB and other short-term rates.
It was initially set to be as high as the upper bound of its target range. But, over time, as
market pressures moved the federal funds rate toward the top of the target range, the Fed
moved the IORB rate a bit lower and within the target range.
</p>

<p align="justify">
* For instance, as of July 14, 2022, with the Fed’s target range being 1.5-1.75% and the federal
funds rate being 1.58%, the bank rate is 1.75%, the ON RRP rate is around 1.55%, and the
IORB rate is 1.65%.

Why has the federal funds rate at times traded below IORB, given that banks could earn
IORB by just depositing with the Fed? A key reason is that not all institutions eligible for
accounts at the Fed earn interest. Government-sponsored entities—Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac
and the Federal Home Loan Banks (FHLBs)—have accounts at the Fed that earn no interest.
So, these institutions are willing to lend funds to a depository institution at an interest rate
below the IORB rate but above zero. When this transaction occurs it is called arbitrage, and
this action brings the federal funds rate close, but not equal, to the IORB rate. Therefore,
IORB is in most cases a (soft) floor of the federal funds rate.
</p>

<p align="justify">
In June 2023, the Secured Overnight Financing Rate (SOFR) replaced the London Inter-Bank
Offered Rate (LIBOR) as the global standard. As its name speaks, SOFR represents overnight rates
collateralized by Treasuries, while the Effective Federal Funds Rate (EFFR) is based on overnight
loans without collateral. As a result, SOFR is slightly lower than EFFR by a few basis points. 
</p>
